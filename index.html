Our primary research investigates the impact of wood smoke air pollution on human reproduction, examining aspects like fertility and potential hereditary markers from exposure. Using rats as a model, pregnant females were exposed in chambers to these pollutants, assessing effects on fetuses, morphology, bone development, and key organs such as the placenta. The research also explores how this exposure impacts germ cells (eggs and sperm) and their function, analyzing its potential link to infertility in adulthood. Methodologies include histology, stereology, and morphometry. Conducted in one of Latin America’s most polluted regions, the study emphasizes critical periods of fetal development.

Our secondary research is understanding of morphology of the endangered Patagonian Huemul deer in different ecological niches and its functional interpretation. Using a multidisciplinary approach between veterinarians, ecologists and engineers, combining efforts between Computed Tomography (CT), 3D Geometric Morphometrics (GM) and Finite Element Analysis (FEA).




Paulo Salinas


Ph.D. in Morphological Sciences
Master’s in Reproductive Biology, specializing in comparative anatomy, reproductive biology, and the effects of environmental pollutants on development.
